## LUX Story: Career Exploration Game

### ABC (Design Phase)

**Act:** Play for 20 minutes. Make 3 choices. See your patterns emerge. Explore one career path.

**Believe:** "This isn't another personality test that boxes me in. It's a game that shows me who I'm becoming through what I choose."

**Care:** Jamal is 17. He's been asked "what do you want to be?" since he was 12. He doesn't know. His guidance counselor gave him a 200-question assessment that said "you're analytical" and showed him a list of engineering jobs. But Jamal doesn't want to be an engineer. He wants to help people. He also likes solving problems. He also likes building things. The test said pick one. The test didn't know what to do with someone who's all of it.

### STORY (Delivery Phase)

**Stage:** It's 3:47 PM. Jamal is sitting in the school library, avoiding going home. His mom asked again this morning: "Have you thought about what you're doing after graduation?" He hasn't. Not really. He's thought about it every day for three years, but he hasn't figured it out. The career fair is next month. He's supposed to have a plan. He doesn't have a plan. He has a list of things he's good at and a list of things he cares about, and they don't match any job description he's ever seen.

**Tension:** Every career tool asks him to know himself first. "What are your interests?" "What are your strengths?" But Jamal doesn't know. He's 17. He's still figuring it out. The tools assume he's already formed. They don't help him become. They just categorize what he already is. And if he doesn't fit the categories, the tool shrugs and says "try again."

**Opportunity:** LUX Story is a game. You don't answer questions about yourself. You make choices in conversations. You help characters solve problems. You build relationships. And as you play, patterns emerge. Not from a test. From your actions. You see yourself reflected in what you choose. And then you see careers that match those patterns. Not "you're analytical, here are 50 engineering jobs." More like: "You helped Maya navigate a difficult conversation, and you solved a technical problem for Devon. Here are careers where both matter."

**Resolution:** Jamal plays for 20 minutes. He makes 5 choices. He sees his pattern: high helping, high analytical, medium building. The game shows him careers he's never heard of: health informatics, community health coordinator, urban planning. He clicks on one. It shows him the skills he already has, the ones he's developing, and the ones he'd need to learn. It shows him Birmingham organizations doing this work. It doesn't tell him what to be. It shows him who he's becoming.

**Yes:** "Play for 20 minutes. Make 3 choices. See what patterns emerge. Then explore one career path that matches."

## Using This Framework for LUX Story

1. **Write the Care first.** Name a person. Make them specific. What keeps them up at night?
   - Jamal: "I don't know who I am yet, but every tool assumes I do."
   - Ms. Rodriguez: "I can't do discovery in 20 minutes. I can only do triage."
   - Marcus's mom: "I want to help, but every attempt feels like pressure."

2. **Care becomes Stage + Tension.** Stage = set the scene, make them feel it. Tension = the conflict or challenge blocking them.
   - Stage: Library, 3:47 PM. Office, 2:15 PM. Kitchen table, 8:30 PM.
   - Tension: Tools assume you're already formed. Can't do discovery in 20 minutes. Pressure vs. space.

3. **Opportunity is your product/program.** Not features. The turning point that breaks the tension.
   - Not "it's a career exploration game."
   - More like: "You make choices. Patterns emerge. Careers match those patterns. Not from a test. From your actions."

4. **Resolution is their life after.** Not your success metrics. Their outcome.
   - Jamal: "He sees careers he's never heard of. It doesn't tell him what to be. It shows him who he's becoming."
   - Ms. Rodriguez: "She walks into conversations with data. Not test results. Actual evidence."
   - Marcus's mom: "The conversation shifts from pressure to curiosity."

5. **Yes is concrete.** Not "learn more." A specific next step they can take today.
   - "Play for 20 minutes. Make 3 choices. See what patterns emerge."
   - "Assign it as a 30-minute activity. Review the dashboard."
   - "Have them play. Get the report. Start the next conversation with curiosity."

## Key Insights for LUX Story Messaging

1. **It's not a test. It's a game.** This removes the pressure. Students aren't being evaluated. They're exploring.

2. **Patterns emerge from actions, not answers.** This is the core differentiator. You don't answer questions about yourself. You make choices. The patterns reveal themselves.

3. **It shows you who you're becoming, not who you are.** This addresses the core tension: students don't know themselves yet. The game helps them discover, not categorize.

4. **It gives educators data, not test results.** The dashboard shows patterns, career interests, skill development. Actionable data for real conversations.

5. **It shifts conversations from pressure to curiosity.** Parents and educators can start with "I see you've been exploring X" instead of "what do you want to be?"
