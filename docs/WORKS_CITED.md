## I. Core Theoretical Frameworks

### Career Development Theory

Bandura, A. (1986). *Social foundations of thought and action: A social cognitive theory*. Prentice-Hall.
> **Application:** Social Cognitive Career Theory (SCCT) explains career development through self-efficacy, outcome expectations, and personal goals. Self-efficacy accounts for 25-35% of variance in career-related performance.

Holland, J. L. (1997). *Making vocational choices: A theory of vocational personalities and work environments* (3rd ed.). Psychological Assessment Resources.
> **Application:** RIASEC theory (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional) maps personality types to career environments. 70% of people fit 2-3 adjacent types.

Super, D. E. (1980). A life-span, life-space approach to career development. *Journal of Vocational Behavior*, 16(3), 282-298.
> **Application:** Career development as lifelong process with exploration, establishment, maintenance, and decline phases.

---

### Identity & Developmental Psychology

Erikson, E. H. (1968). *Identity: Youth and crisis*. W.W. Norton & Company.
> **Application:** Adolescence (ages 12-18) is the critical period for "Identity vs. Role Confusion" - the psychosocial stage where career identity forms.

McAdams, D. P. (2001). The psychology of life stories. *Review of General Psychology*, 5(2), 100-122.
> **Application:** Narrative Identity Theory - coherent life narratives predict career satisfaction and resilience better than traditional career assessments.

Marcia, J. E. (1966). Development and validation of ego-identity status. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 3(5), 551-558.
> **Application:** Identity statuses (diffusion, foreclosure, moratorium, achievement) describe how adolescents explore and commit to identities.

---

### Learning & Cognition

Csíkszentmihályi, M. (1990). *Flow: The psychology of optimal experience*. Harper & Row.
> **Application:** Flow states occur when challenge matches skill level, increasing learning retention by ~40%.

Sweller, J. (1988). Cognitive load during problem solving: Effects on learning. *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 257-285.
> **Application:** Cognitive Load Theory distinguishes intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load. Reducing extraneous load improves learning up to 40%.

Piaget, J. (1952). *The origins of intelligence in children*. International Universities Press.
> **Application:** Constructivist learning - knowledge is built through experience, not transmitted passively.

Kolb, D. A. (1984). *Experiential learning: Experience as the source of learning and development*. Prentice-Hall.
> **Application:** Learning cycle (concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation).

---

### Motivation & Engagement

Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2000). The "what" and "why" of goal pursuits: Human needs and the self-determination of behavior. *Psychological Inquiry*, 11(4), 227-268.
> **Application:** Self-Determination Theory identifies autonomy, competence, and relatedness as essential for intrinsic motivation. Autonomy-supportive environments increase motivation 25-30%.

Immordino-Yang, M. H., & Damasio, A. (2007). We feel, therefore we learn: The relevance of affective and social neuroscience to education. *Mind, Brain, and Education*, 1(1), 3-10.
> **Application:** Limbic Learning Theory - emotion and cognition are intertwined. Emotionally engaged learners show 60% better retention.

## II. Assessment & Measurement

### Validity & Evidence-Based Assessment

Messick, S. (1995). Validity of psychological assessment: Validation of inferences from persons' responses and performances as scientific inquiry into score meaning. *American Psychologist*, 50(9), 741-749.
> **Application:** Evidence-based assessment emphasizes multiple sources of evidence. Performance-based assessments show 60% higher predictive validity than self-report.

American Psychiatric Association. (2013). *Diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders* (5th ed.). American Psychiatric Publishing.
> **Application:** DSM-5 neurocognitive domains framework adapted for skill assessment: Complex Attention, Executive Functions, Learning & Memory, Language, Perceptual-Motor, Social Cognition.

---

### Behavioral Assessment

Azjen, I. (1991). The theory of planned behavior. *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 50(2), 179-211.
> **Application:** Behavior predicted by attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. Behavioral observation outperforms intention measurement.

Baumeister, R. F., Vohs, K. D., & Funder, D. C. (2007). Psychology as the science of self-reports and finger movements: Whatever happened to actual behavior? *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 2(4), 396-403.
> **Application:** Critique of over-reliance on self-report. Argues for behavioral observation as gold standard.

## IV. Game Design & Interactive Media

### Game Design Theory

Koster, R. (2004). *A theory of fun for game design*. Paraglyph Press.
> **Application:** Fun emerges from learning and pattern recognition. Games are "teachers in disguise."

Schell, J. (2008). *The art of game design: A book of lenses*. CRC Press.
> **Application:** 100+ design "lenses" for evaluating game experiences. Experience over mechanics.

Hunicke, R., LeBlanc, M., & Zubek, R. (2004). MDA: A formal approach to game design and game research. *Proceedings of the AAAI Workshop on Challenges in Game AI*, 4(1), 1-5.
> **Application:** Mechanics-Dynamics-Aesthetics framework separating rules, behavior, and emotional response.

---

### Narrative Game Design

Keenan, J. (2021). Writing Disco Elysium [GDC Talk]. Game Developers Conference.

Gallagher, R. (2012). Story structure and the structure of stories. In *Game writing: Narrative skills for videogames* (2nd ed.). Charles River Media.
> **Application:** Branching narrative architecture and player agency in story games.

Murray, J. H. (1997). *Hamlet on the holodeck: The future of narrative in cyberspace*. Free Press.
